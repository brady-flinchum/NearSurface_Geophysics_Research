Welcome
-------

I am a near-surface geophysicist who uses geophysical methods to characterise
the critical zone (CZ)—the layer of the Earth that extends from treetops to
bedrock and supports all terrestrial life. The CZ, where regolith forms, is
inherently interdisciplinary and draws together researchers from across the
Earth sciences. My research focuses on bridging gaps between measurements that
span wide temporal and spatial scales, using the unique imaging capabilities of
geophysics to develop new perspectives on the physical and chemical processes
that create regolith. I specialise in shallow seismic refraction, but I also
have experience with ground-penetrating radar (GPR), seismic reflection, direct
current (DC) electrical resistivity, and surface and borehole nuclear magnetic
resonance (NMR).

Since beginning my PhD in 2013, I have been deeply committed to mentoring
undergraduate and graduate students. I value students' perspectives, time, and
life commitments, and I strive to foster a supportive and safe working
environment.
